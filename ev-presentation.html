🇩🇪 Berlin · Site Visit

CityLAB Berlin

A public innovation lab housed in Tempelhof Airport, operating at the intersection of government, civil society, and technology.

🧪
Prototyping-First Philosophy
Low-stakes MVPs pitched to government partners. Iterate quickly, measure adoption, scale what works. No large-scale system proposals upfront.
📊
Open Data Culture
All source code published openly. Projects include urban tree health, air quality tracking, and administrative process tools — building institutional trust through transparency.
🚗
Freemove Project
Anonymization methods for mobility data supporting sustainable urban planning — the kind of infrastructure that underpins demand-responsive EV network deployment.
💡 CityLAB mirrors Berlin's broader EV ecosystem: pilot projects, public-private testbeds, and incremental scaling rather than top-down mandates.
03 / 10
🇩🇪 Berlin · Site Visit

EUREF-Campus

A live laboratory for the energy transition on a former gasworks site — the most directly relevant site of our Berlin fieldwork.

Public EV Charging Hub
Germany's largest electric charging station. 12 Tesla Superchargers + multiple charging options. Anyone can drive on and charge.
Battery Second-Life Program
Audi engineers testing stationary energy storage from retired EV batteries. Closed-loop model addressing long-term sustainability.
Lamppost-to-Charger Conversion
Converting existing lampposts into EV charging stations in under one hour — a scalability lever pioneered by Berlin-based Ubitricity.
CO₂-Neutral* Energy
Renewable sources + innovative storage. *CHP plant runs partly on natural gas, offset through a credit system. Net zero, not zero emissions.
"Despite the sophistication of the infrastructure, Uber drivers showed little interest in EVs — the city's exceptional public transit reduces urgency for private EV adoption."
— On-site observation, EUREF-Campus
⚠️ Infrastructure is scaling, but demand-side drivers remain uneven — especially among margin-driven ride-hail and delivery workers.

🇩🇪 Berlin · Observation

East Side Gallery

A high-footfall corridor revealing the gap between micro-mobility demand and EV infrastructure deployment.

🚲
What We Saw
E-bikes parked densely throughout the corridor. Berlin has clearly embraced e-bike and shared mobility culture. The area is heavily trafficked by tourists, cyclists, and locals.
🔌
What Was Missing
Public EV charging stations were notably sparse. A prioritization question emerges: the city's EV charging deployment in mixed-use corridors lags behind micro-mobility.
🧱
The East/West Hypothesis
The East Side Gallery sits in what was formerly East Berlin. The uneven distribution of post-reunification investment may be partially legible in the density and quality of charging infrastructure across different parts of the city today.

This raises a deeper question about whether Berlin's structural advantage is evenly distributed — or concentrated in the west and in showcase campuses like EUREF.

🇨🇿 Prague · User Interview

EV Charging on the Ground

A local EV driver's perspective reveals the daily reality of Prague's fragmented charging ecosystem.

📍
Periphery-Concentrated
Near-zero public chargers in the city center. Most stations at shopping centers (Lidl, malls) on the outskirts. Free charging available but frequently occupied.
🔀
Fragmented Networks
PRE, EON, CEZ, and others all require different cards and apps. Some stations can't simultaneously charge two vehicles through different plug types.
⏱️
Overstay Penalties
PRE hyperchargers charge per-minute overstay fees after charging completes — punishing drivers who can't immediately move their vehicles.
🏛️ Historic preservation, narrow streets, and aging utility infrastructure have pushed EV charging toward the urban edge rather than integrating it into the city's daily movement patterns.

🇨🇿 Prague · Observation

The Škoda Moment

A small interaction that captured where Prague currently sits in its EV transition.

"A hotel staff member, when asked about nearby EV charging, admitted he had no idea where to find one — and pointed us instead to a news article about Prague's plan to convert lampposts into chargers by 2030."
— He drives a Škoda.
🔑
Three Layers Exposed
Policy level: Infrastructure ambition exists — 6,000 lamppost chargers planned.

Street level: Cultural attachment to domestic combustion-engine vehicles (Škoda) remains strong.

The gap: The two have not yet met in any meaningful way in daily urban life.

🔍 Reflection

What Immersion Confirmed
& Challenged

✅
Confirmed
Berlin's post-reunification modernization creates a real structural advantage for EV deployment. EUREF-Campus made this tangible — V2G integration, battery second-life systems, and renewable micro-grids operating within a single urban block.
✅
Confirmed
Prague's historic preservation requirements genuinely constrain infrastructure deployment. The gap between policy ambition and street-level reality is wider than secondary research suggested.
🔄
Complicated
Berlin's advantage is not evenly distributed. The East Side Gallery observation suggests post-reunification investment may be concentrated in western districts and showcase campuses. The East/West divide persists.
🔄
Challenged
Even in Berlin's most advanced EV infrastructure, demand-side adoption lags. Uber drivers' disinterest in EVs shows that building supply doesn't automatically generate demand — especially for margin-sensitive workers.
🎯 Key takeaway: The gap between these cities is not just hardware — it's governance models, data infrastructure, institutional culture, and the willingness to integrate vs. restrict new mobility technology.
