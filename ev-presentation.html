📋 Regulatory Deep Dive

The EU Mandate Challenge

Both cities must comply with EU Alternative Fuels Infrastructure Regulation (AFIR) — but their starting points are vastly different.

🇩🇪
Berlin's Regulatory Stack
• Federal: Masterplan Ladeinfrastruktur II — national charging strategy

• State: Berlin Mobilitätsgesetz — legally embeds sustainable transport

• Municipal: Building codes require EV-ready parking in new developments

• Innovation: CityLAB provides open-data tools for demand-responsive planning
🇨🇿
Prague's Regulatory Stack
• National: National Action Plan for Clean Mobility — targets exist but underfunded

• Municipal: Heritage protection overrides most infrastructure proposals in Zone 1

• Market: No interoperability mandate across PRE/EON/CEZ networks

• Adaptation: 6,000 lamppost charger target by 2030 — the primary scaling strategy
(1 min): "Berlin has a multi-level regulatory stack supporting EV deployment — federal strategy, state mobility law, municipal building codes. Prague has ambition at the national level but the municipal reality is dominated by heritage protection. The lamppost strategy is Prague's best regulatory workaround: it doesn't require excavation or permanent infrastructure changes, which means it can potentially bypass heritage objections."

👷 Workforce Dynamics

Who Builds the Network?

EV infrastructure deployment requires specialized technical labor — and both cities face different workforce realities.

🇩🇪
Berlin: Ecosystem Depth
• EUREF-Campus functions as a live training ground — Audi engineers, energy researchers, and EV specialists working on-site

• Germany's dual education system (Ausbildung) produces certified electricians at scale

• Ubitricity, Compleo, and other German EV-tech companies provide specialized installation workforces

• CityLAB cultivates data science and urban planning talent for smart grid applications
🇨🇿
Prague: Emerging Capacity
• Škoda Auto's Enyaq iV production creates a growing domestic EV manufacturing workforce

• However, charger installation requires different skills than vehicle manufacturing

• Limited specialized EV infrastructure training programs compared to Germany

• Heritage-compliant installation requires additional specialized skills (historic masonry, period-appropriate concealment)
🔧 Key gap: Prague has auto manufacturing workforce (Škoda), but building cars ≠ installing chargers. The city needs to grow a specialized installation and maintenance workforce, particularly for heritage-sensitive contexts.
(1 min): "Workforce is an underappreciated factor. Germany's dual education system produces certified electricians at scale. Berlin has companies like Ubitricity training dedicated installation teams. Prague has Škoda — but building EVs and installing chargers are different skill sets. And heritage-compliant installation requires additional specialized skills that don't exist at scale yet. This is a bottleneck that will slow deployment even if funding and permits are solved."

🇩🇪 Social Factors

Berlin: E-Bike Culture,
EV-Curious

🚲
Micro-Mobility Adoption
E-bikes are ubiquitous across Berlin. The East Side Gallery corridor showed dense e-bike parking alongside pedestrians and tourists. The city has culturally embraced electric personal mobility.
🚇
Transit Paradox
Berlin's exceptional public transit (U-Bahn, S-Bahn, trams, buses) actually reduces urgency for private EV adoption. Why own an EV when you have a €49/month Deutschlandticket?
🚗
Demand-Side Gap
Despite 70K registered EVs, Uber and delivery drivers at EUREF showed little EV interest — margins are too tight. Infrastructure supply is outpacing demand among professional drivers.
"The infrastructure is scaling, but demand-side drivers remain uneven — particularly among margin-driven ride-hail and delivery workers."
— EUREF-Campus observation
(1 min): "Berlin's social landscape is interesting because it's contradictory. E-bike culture is massive — people are comfortable with electric mobility. But private EV adoption is complicated by excellent public transit. Why own a car at all? And among professional drivers who do need vehicles, EVs don't make economic sense yet. So Berlin has a supply-side success story but a demand-side question mark."

🇨🇿 Social Factors

Prague: The Škoda Identity

Where Berlin's culture is e-bike-forward, Prague's automotive identity is deeply tied to a domestic combustion-engine brand.

"A hotel staff member, asked about EV charging, had no idea where to find one — and pointed us to a news article about Prague's lamppost charger plan. He drives a Škoda."
— Fieldwork interaction, Prague
🏭
Škoda's Dual Role
Škoda is Czechia's largest private employer and a source of national pride. Its Enyaq iV EV shows the brand is transitioning — but the cultural association remains combustion-first. This creates both a barrier and an opportunity.
📱
Awareness Gap
Policy level: Ambition exists (6,000 lamppost chargers by 2030).

Street level: Hotel staff, taxi drivers, and residents largely unaware of existing infrastructure.

The gap: Policy and daily life haven't connected yet.
🚇
Transit Alternative
Prague's metro + tram network is efficient and affordable. Like Berlin, strong public transit reduces the urgency for private EV ownership — but unlike Berlin, the cultural baseline is combustion, not electric.
(1 min): "The Škoda moment really captured this. The hotel employee — a daily driver — had zero awareness of existing charging infrastructure. Škoda IS the Czech car. It's building EVs now (Enyaq iV), but the cultural association is still combustion. This matters because consumer demand drives private investment. If people don't see themselves as EV drivers, the demand signal stays weak, and the business case for new chargers stays thin."

🎯 Strategic Insights

Three Core Findings

1. Infrastructure ≠ Adoption
Berlin proves that building chargers doesn't automatically drive EV adoption. Uber drivers at EUREF-Campus showed zero interest despite world-class infrastructure meters away. The demand side requires its own strategy: subsidies, fleet mandates, total-cost-of-ownership education. Supply without demand creates expensive ghost infrastructure.
2. Constraint Breeds Innovation (Sometimes)
Prague's lamppost conversion strategy emerged precisely because conventional deployment is blocked by heritage rules. This constraint-driven innovation could leapfrog traditional station-based models — if executed at scale. The 6,000 lamppost target by 2030 is Prague's most viable path forward.
3. The Gap Is Governance, Not Geography
The Berlin-Prague divide isn't primarily about wealth or technology — it's about institutional culture. Berlin's government experiments and iterates (CityLAB). Prague's government restricts and studies. This governance gap shapes everything downstream: what gets funded, what gets permitted, and what gets built. Changing institutional culture is harder than installing chargers.
(2 min): "Three core findings. First: infrastructure alone doesn't drive adoption — Berlin shows this clearly. Second: Prague's constraints are actually generating innovative solutions — the lamppost model could leapfrog traditional deployment. Third, and most important: the fundamental difference between these cities isn't money or technology. It's how their institutions respond to change. Berlin experiments. Prague restricts. Until Prague's governance culture shifts, even good policy will struggle to translate into deployed infrastructure."

💡 Recommendations

Strategic Implications

What each city should prioritize based on our analysis.

For Berlin
Close the Demand Gap
Shift focus from infrastructure build-out to demand-side activation. Fleet electrification mandates for ride-hail and delivery. Expand total-cost-of-ownership tools for gig workers. Address the East/West deployment imbalance.
For Prague
Accelerate Lamppost Deployment
Double down on the lamppost conversion strategy — it's Prague's only heritage-compatible path at scale. Mandate interoperability across PRE/EON/CEZ networks via a unified payment standard. Create a "Prague CityLAB" equivalent for mobility data.
For Prague
Leverage Škoda's Transition
Partner with Škoda's Enyaq iV marketing to shift cultural association from combustion to electric. Use national pride as a vector for EV adoption — "drive Czech, drive electric." Bundle charger installation with Enyaq purchases.
For Both
Adopt EU AFIR as a Floor, Not a Ceiling
EU mandates set minimum charging density targets. Both cities should exceed these to build consumer confidence. Berlin should use AFIR compliance to address East/West gaps. Prague should use AFIR deadlines to overcome institutional inertia.
(2 min): "Four recommendations. For Berlin: stop building and start selling — demand-side activation is the priority. For Prague: the lamppost model is your best bet, but you need network interoperability and a cultural shift leveraging Škoda's own EV transition. For both: use EU AFIR as a forcing function to overcome internal resistance and geographic imbalances."
